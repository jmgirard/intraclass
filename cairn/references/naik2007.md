## What it adds over young1998

`young1998` tests **two** populations assuming **equal variances**. This paper
tests **`g`** populations with **variances free to differ** — and the motivation
is concrete rather than formal. Hanley (2004) recovered Galton's family-stature
notebooks; comparing the sib–sib correlation among boys in one family with that
among girls in another means the two groups are boys and girls, and "there is no
reason to believe that the two variances of the two populations are equal"
(p. 6499).

**The overlap with `young1998`, stated once for both notes.** The two papers
share: the familial one-way compound-symmetric normal model; **Srivastava's
(1984) estimator** reached by a Helmert transformation; unequal family sizes by
conditional analysis; truncated-negative-binomial simulated family sizes at
success probability 0.483 with 10,000 replications; and a `ρ` range admitting
negative values. They differ in five ways worth keeping straight:

| | `young1998` | `naik2007` |
|---|---|---|
| Populations | 2 | `g` (simulated at 2 and 3) |
| Variances | `σ²₁ = σ²₂` assumed | free to differ |
| Tests | 3 (LRT, `Z`, `Z*`) | 5 (LRT, Score, Wald, `T₀`, `T₁`) |
| Estimator in the tests | Srivastava throughout | ML for the first three, Srivastava for `T₀`/`T₁` |
| Recommendation | the LRT | the **score test or `T₀`** |
| Helmert first row | `1/√p_i` | `1/m_ij` — so `y_ij1` is the sib **average** (p. 6501) |
| Family-size distribution | mean 2.86, 2–15 siblings | mean 2.84, 1–15 children, citing Brass (1958) |

The two papers therefore reach **opposite practical recommendations**, and the
reason is not taste — see the negative-LRT finding below.

## The five tests

Three are likelihood-based, each asymptotically `χ²` with `g − 1` df, with ML
fitted by SAS IML optimization (p. 6501): the **LRT** (Eq. 1, p. 6500), **Rao's
score test** (Eq. 2, p. 6500, with the score components and the block-diagonal
`3×3`-per-population Fisher information printed on pp. 6500–6501), and **Wald's
test** (Eq. 4, p. 6501, using the contrast matrix `C` of Eq. 3).

Two are the paper's own proposals, built "in the spirit of Young and Bhandary
(1998)" on Srivastava's estimator and its asymptotic variance: **`T₀`** (Eq. 10)
evaluates the variance–covariance matrix **under `H₀`**, **`T₁`** (Eq. 11)
without restriction (p. 6504). Both are `χ²_{g−1}`.

**Estimator efficiency has a crossover at `ρ ≈ 0.3`** (p. 6502): Srivastava &
Katapa (1986) showed `ρ̂_i` is a form of Smith's (1957) uniform-weight estimator
whose asymptotic variance is close to the MLE's "only if the intraclass
correlation is greater than about 0.3", while a family-size-weighted estimator
does better below it. Srivastava (1993) combines the two as
`ρ̂_Si = ρ̂_wi/(1 + ρ̂_wi − ρ̂_ui)` (Eqs. 6–7, p. 6502) for higher efficiency than
either alone. The standard error of the pooled `ρ̂_S` (Eq. 9, p. 6503) has no
tractable expression because its weights are random, so the paper **uses the
bootstrap** for it in the worked example.

## The finding that matters most

Substituting Srivastava's estimators into likelihood-based test expressions —
which is what `young1998` does — "may lead to difficulties **unless of course,
one uses the distribution of the modified test statistics as in Konishi and
Gupta (1989)**" (p. 6503), the escape route being `konishi1989`'s weighted-sum
null rather than a naive `χ²`. The paper reports (p. 6503):

> thus modified (negative two times the) likelihood ratio was negative,
> sometimes, for as much as 25% of the simulated data sets.

A negative `−2 log Λ` is not a small-sample inefficiency; it is a statistic
outside its own support, in up to a quarter of samples. The paper allows that
"since Srivastava's estimators are CAN estimators, for really large samples the
modified likelihood ratio may approximate the true likelihood ratio" — but this
is a direct, quantified caution against the earlier paper's headline
recommendation, and the pair should not be cited as agreeing.

## Simulation and recommendation (§6, pp. 6504–6508)

`g = 2` with `(n₁, n₂) = (25, 35)`; `g = 3` with `(25, 30, 35)`; `μ = 0`;
Case 1 `σ²₁ = 1, σ²₂ = 2`; Case 2 `σ²₁ = 1, σ²₂ = 2, σ²₃ = 3`; `ρ` over 0.1–0.9
in steps of 0.1; 10,000 simulations. Tables 1–3 (pp. 6505–6506) are sizes at
`α = 0.01, 0.05, 0.10`; Tables 4–9 are powers; Figs. 1–2 plot the score-vs-`T₀`
power curves.

The four stated observations (p. 6505): the `χ²` approximation is poor for
**Wald** in the tails; **`T₁` is "very erratic"**, above nominal at some `ρ` and
below at others; the **LRT**'s sizes run "generally slightly larger than the
assumed level"; and only the **score test and `T₀`** hold the nominal level,
though generally conservative. Power is then compared only between those two,
since only they are correctly sized — the same like-for-like discipline
`bhandary2006` applies. Score edges `T₀` consistently but narrowly.

The recommendation (p. 6508): **the score test or `T₀`**, with a practical lean
toward `T₀` because it "can be computed directly without going through numerical
evaluations". The score test's edge is attributed to its being locally most
powerful (Mukherjee 1993), and `T₀`'s closeness to the fact that it, too,
evaluates its variance matrix under `H₀`.

## Galton's data (§5, p. 6504)

Hanley (2004) via Galton (1886, 1889): 205 families split into 102
daughters-only and 103 sons-only, testing whether the sib–sib correlation
differs between daughters and sons. **All five tests fail to reject** at
`α = 0.05` (`P` from 0.36 to 0.41). Estimates and standard errors are tabulated
on p. 6504 and not reproduced here — this note is boundary evidence, not an
oracle source. The one methodological detail worth carrying: the pooled
Srivastava estimator's standard error is obtained by **bootstrap**, because its
random weights make a closed-form expression intractable (p. 6503).

## Boundary (IP2)

**Testing whether ICCs are equal across populations is outside this package's
contract**, at any `g` and with or without an equal-variance assumption.
`intraclass` estimates interrater ICCs and reports boundary-aware intervals for
them; a `g`-sample homogeneity test is a different inferential target. Adopting
one would require an **IP2 constitutional amendment — a D-entry plus an explicit
user decision — not a feature request.** As with `young1998`, the familial
one-way design carries no rater facet, so the distance from the contract is
twofold.

## Open questions

- **No `ρ = 0` cell**, as everywhere in this cluster: the grid is 0.1–0.9. The
  `ρ ≈ 0.3` efficiency crossover (p. 6502) means the near-zero region is exactly
  where the estimator choice matters most, and it is the region least simulated.
- **`T₁`'s erratic sizing is reported, not explained.** The paper drops it from
  the power comparison without diagnosing why the unrestricted variance estimate
  misbehaves.
- **The 25 % negative-LRT rate is not broken down.** It is given as a single
  aggregate over "the simulated data sets", with no indication of which `ρ`,
  `g`, or family-size cells produce it — so it cannot be used to bound the
  `young1998` procedure's validity region, only to flag that one exists.
- **Small `g` only.** `g = 2` and `g = 3` are simulated; the tests are stated for
  general `g` with `χ²_{g−1}` calibration, untested beyond three populations.
