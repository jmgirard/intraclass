## Design applicability (AC2 — this is the important section)

| Axis | What the paper covers |
|---|---|
| Domain | **Familial correlation, not interrater reliability.** ρ measures "the degree of intrafamily resemblance with respect to characteristics such as blood pressure, cholesterol level, weight, height" (p. 111) |
| Structure | **One-way / exchangeable**, not two-way. Each family is one cluster with a compound-symmetric covariance (Eq. 1, p. 112); there is no rater facet and no crossed second factor |
| Samples | **Multi-sample.** `K` populations, `p_i` families in population `i`; the estimand is the ρ *common to all K populations*, under `H₀: ρ₁ = … = ρ_K` |
| Balance | **Unbalanced by construction** — "samples of unbalanced families with unequal sizes" (p. 112); family sizes `n_ij` vary and are simulated from a fitted size distribution |
| Modification | **None.** Plain profile likelihood, `κ = 0` in `xiao2013`'s notation |

**This is a different estimand from the package's.** Mapping it onto the
package's vocabulary, a single population (`K = 1`) with families as clusters is
the **one-way random ICC(1,1)** with unequal cluster sizes; the multi-sample
common-ρ layer on top has no package analogue at all. It is *not* the two-way
interrater design of `xiao2013`, despite the shared first author and the
near-identical title language.

## The method

Notation (p. 112): `K` populations; population `i` has `p_i` families; family `j`
has `n_ij` individuals, observed as `X_ij = (X_ij,1, …, X_ij,n_ij)ᵀ ~
N(μ_i 1_{n_ij}, Σ_ij)`, with **Eq. (1)** giving the compound-symmetric
`Σ_ij = σ²_i × [1 on the diagonal, ρ_i off-diagonal]`. `μ_i`, `σ_i`, `ρ_i` are
the common mean, common SD, and common correlation of population `i`. Families
are independent; sample sizes and family sizes may vary. p. 113 notes it "may be
reasonable to further assume the values of `ρ_i (i = 1, 2, …, K)` are
non-negative, particular in the analysis of familial data" — **a non-negativity
assumption, stated as an option, not enforced**. (The parenthesized index list is
printed in the source and was dropped from this quotation until M71 review;
confirmed at 400 DPI, the text layer mangling the subscripts.)

Sufficient statistics, Eqs. (2)–(4), p. 113: `X̄_ij` (family mean), `S²_ij =
Σ_k (X_ij,k − X̄_ij)²` (family SS, *not* divided by `n_ij − 1`), and the weight
`g_ij(ρ_i) = n_ij / [1 + (n_ij − 1)ρ_i]`.

**Eq. (6), p. 113** — the per-family `−2l`, credited to Mian and Shoukri (1997):

```
−2l_ij = n_ij ln(2πσ²_i) + (n_ij − 1) ln(1 − ρ_i) + ln(1 + (n_ij − 1)ρ_i)
         + (1/σ²_i)[ S²_ij/(1 − ρ_i) + g_ij(ρ_i)(X̄_ij − μ_i)² ]
```

with `−2l = −2ΣᵢΣⱼ l_ij` (Eq. 5). The nuisance parameters have **closed-form**
conditional MLEs given `ρ_i` — this is what makes the method cheap:

- **Eq. (7):** `μ̂_i(ρ_i) = Σⱼ g_ij(ρ_i) X̄_ij / Σⱼ g_ij(ρ_i)` — a
  precision-weighted mean, larger families down-weighted as `ρ` grows.
- **Eq. (8):** `σ̂²_i(ρ_i) = (1/Σⱼ n_ij) Σⱼ [ S²_ij/(1 − ρ_i) +
  g_ij(ρ_i)(X̄_ij − μ̂_i(ρ_i))² ]`.

**Eq. (9), p. 114:** substituting (7) and (8) into (5) gives
`l(ρ) = ΣᵢΣⱼ l_ij(ρ, μ̂_i(ρ), σ̂²_i(ρ))` — "a function of just one variable".

**Eq. (10), p. 114** — the interval, with **no `κ` correction**:
`{ ρ ∈ (0,1) : −2(l(ρ) − l(ρ̂)) ≤ χ²_{1,α} }`, bounds solving **Eq. (11)**
`−2(l(ρ) − l(ρ̂)) = χ²_{1,α}`. Implementation guidance (p. 114): Brent's method
or bisection, with **the lower bound bracketed in `(0, ρ̂)` and the upper bound
in `(ρ̂, 1)`** — the same one-dimensional root-finding-over-optimization shape
`xiao2013` describes, but one level cheaper because (7)/(8) are closed form.

Eqs. (12)–(13), p. 114 give the equality test `H₀: ρ₁ = … = ρ_K`; the paper's
crude rule is to reject if any per-population MLE falls outside the common-ρ
interval. (This is the same territory as the M67 equality-testing cluster —
`young1998` and `bhandary2006` are cited here for exactly that problem.)

### The finding that matters most here: naive PL does *not* under-cover in this design

**Every one of the 36 PL cells lands in 0.931–0.950 against a nominal 0.95** —
including `ρ = 0.1`, the low-ρ region. Compare `xiao2013`, where the *same*
first author found naive PL (`κ = 0`) bottoming out at **0.796** against a
nominal 0.90 and needed the `κ_m` correction to fix it. The paper's own summary
(pp. 115, 117): PL "obviously performs better in most cases", the PL intervals
"have better coverage as well as precision (or shorter lengths) for low values
of `ρ` than that using generalized pivots", and GP "occasionally wins for a
couple of high values of `ρ`" — tempered by the Summary's closing line, "Even
for high values of `ρ`, the methodology presented here does not perform less
well" (p. 117).

**The low-`ρ` claim is visible in the table, not just the prose.** GP's worst
cell is `0.913` (`K = 3`, 25 families, `ρ = 0.1`) and its **two** lowest cells
sit at `ρ ∈ {0.1, 0.2}`, while PL never drops below 0.931 anywhere. GP's full
range is 0.913–0.947 against PL's 0.931–0.950. Sorting all 36 GP coverage cells
ascending gives 0.913 (`ρ = 0.1`), 0.919 (`ρ = 0.2`), 0.923 (`ρ = 0.5`),
0.925 (`ρ = 0.3`), 0.925 (`ρ = 0.9`) — **all five in the `K = 3`, 25-family
block**, so the sharper reading is that GP's weakness tracks the *most
nuisance-parameter-dense* design, not `ρ` alone, with the low-`ρ` corner worst
inside it. That block is **not the smallest**: p. 115 sets "the number of populations is
`K = 2, 3` with `p₁ = p₂ = ⋯ = p_K` = 25 or 50 families from each population",
so `K = 3` at 25 each is 75 families against
`K = 2` at 25 each = 50. What it maximizes is populations — and p. 113 names
`μ_i` and `σ_i` as **the nuisance parameters** (`ρ` itself being common across
populations, which is the estimand), so `K = 3` carries 6 nuisance parameters
against `K = 2`'s 4, each estimated from the smaller of the two family counts.
The signature is incidental-parameters, not smallness. Two earlier readings corrected at M71
review: the claim that the four lowest cells were all at low `ρ` (only two are),
and the description of this block as the smallest design.

**So "profile likelihood under-covers for ICCs" is design-specific, not a
property of profile likelihood.** The difference is the nuisance-parameter
count: here `(μ_i, σ_i)` are profiled out in closed form (Eqs. 7–8) and the
paper attributes PL's good behavior to Mian & Shoukri's observation that the ML
equation for ρ "is an optimal estimating equation in the sense of Godambe
(1960)" (p. 115); in `xiao2013` the crossed rater facet leaves `ρ_r` to be
profiled numerically and the χ² calibration degrades as `S` grows. This is the
concrete vindication of the M62 implement-gate split.

## Worked example (§4, pp. 115–116)

Miall & Oldham (1955) arterial blood pressure, previously analyzed by Donner &
Koval (1980) and Mian & Shoukri (1997). Propositi with no siblings and with at
most one child excluded, leaving **218 families**, split by gender and age
(cut at 40 years) into four groups: young female 62, elder female 36, young male
75, elder male 45.

- Common ρ̂ = **0.0748** (systolic), 95 % CI **(0.0314, 0.1272)**;
- Common ρ̂ = **0.1163** (diastolic), 95 % CI **(0.0648, 0.1763)**.

**Table 2, p. 116** — per-group estimates, which the paper reads as showing the
groups do *not* in fact share a common ICC:

| | Young female | Elder female | Young male | Elder male |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Systolic | 0.0760 | 0.3027 | 0.0838 | 0.0028 |
| Diastolic | 0.0832 | 0.2468 | 0.0663 | 0.1433 |

Note the near-boundary `0.0028` estimate — the paper reports it without comment
and does not say whether the optimizer was constrained to `ρ ≥ 0`.

## Open questions

- **Not usable for the PL sibling pass as a primary source.** The candidate row
  names `xiao2013`; this paper covers a different design and should not be cited
  for two-way interrater claims. Its role is the contrast finding above.
- **`S²_ij` scaling.** Eq. (3) defines `S²_ij` as a raw sum of squares with no
  `n_ij − 1` divisor, and Eqs. (6)/(8) use it that way. Anyone reimplementing
  must not substitute a sample variance. Recorded because it is the kind of
  silent factor-of-`(n−1)` that a reimplementation gets wrong.
- **No non-normal or near-zero-boundary evidence.** Simulation is Gaussian
  throughout and the lowest ρ cell is 0.1, not 0. The `ρ = 0` boundary — where
  this package's one-way default is known to abort (M62/RR01) — is untested here,
  though 0.1 is far closer to it than anything in `xiao2013`.
- **Two-sided 95 % here vs 90 % in `xiao2013`.** The nominal levels differ
  between the two papers' tables; do not pool them into one comparison.
- **Software by request only** (p. 117): "The approach was implemented by the
  authors and the package can be obtained from the authors upon request." As with
  `xiao2013`, no archived code — no live-implementation oracle available.
- **Bhandary co-authorship — compared, and the family-size designs are close but
  not identical.** `bhandary2006` (same milestone) shares author Bhandary and
  simulates unequal family sizes from the same familial-correlation tradition,
  but the two parameterizations differ and trace to different sources: here a
  **zero-truncated negative binomial** with `m = 2.84`, `P = 0.93` (Brass 1958,
  via Mian & Shoukri 1997), `+1` and truncated at 15; there a negative binomial
  with **mean 2.86**, truncated to a minimum of 2 and maximum of 15 (Rosner et
  al. 1977; Srivastava & Keen 1988). Do not treat results from the two as drawn
  from a common design. Both are Gaussian, exchangeable-within-family, and
  unbalanced; the estimands differ (common-ρ interval here, equality test there).
